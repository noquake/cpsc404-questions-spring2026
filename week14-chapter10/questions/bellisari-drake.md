Chapter 10 frames workplace friction less as direct conflict and more as breakdowns in how concerns are communicated upward, and whether that communication preserves trust. It emphasizes tools like structured one-on-ones and reflective listening, along with the “malice versus stupidity” lens, which encourages assuming lack of awareness or differing perspective before assuming negative intent.

So consider this scenario: your manager regularly takes credit for the team’s work in high-visibility settings, such as leadership presentations, client updates, and internal communications, often describing outcomes as their own accomplishments without acknowledging the individuals who actually produced the work. Over time, team members begin to feel invisible, and motivation starts to decline as effort feels less connected to recognition or ownership. Using the “malice versus stupidity” lens from Chapter 10, how do you use structured one-on-one meetings and reflective listening to raise this pattern in a way that assumes positive intent but still clearly surfaces the impact on morale and engagement, and how can you frame the conversation so it moves from a personal issue about credit to a broader discussion about team motivation, retention risk, and the long-term cost of reduced ownership and visibility on performance?
