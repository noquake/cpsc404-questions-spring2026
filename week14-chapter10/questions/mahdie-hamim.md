# Week 14 Question - Chapter 10: Managing Your Manager

## Question

Chapter 10 suggests that effectively managing your manager involves things like regular one-on-one meetings, reflective listening, and even telling your boss they’re wrong—while also assuming “stupidity over malice” to keep interactions constructive. But in practice, especially in internships, these strategies seem to rely heavily on the assumption that your manager is willing to update their beliefs if you communicate well enough.

In my experience, I’ve seen situations where a manager is consistently wrong but confident, and reflective listening or careful framing doesn’t actually change their decision—it just makes the disagreement more polite. At the same time, directly telling them they’re wrong can feel risky when they control your evaluation.

So how do you distinguish between a situation where better communication (e.g., reflective listening, clearer framing in 1:1s) will actually change your manager’s mind versus one where the outcome is effectively fixed regardless of how well you “manage up”? Does Chapter 10 overestimate how much influence employees realistically have, or is the real skill knowing when these strategies will work versus when you’re just managing the optics of disagreement?
