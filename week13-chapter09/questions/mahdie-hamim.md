Chapter 9 presents “special cases” like micromanagers, flat organizations, remote work, and truly unethical managers as situations that require adapting your usual “manage up” strategies. But what stood out to me is that these cases often break the core assumption behind the book—that your manager is at least somewhat rational and responsive to better communication.

In my experience, especially in internships, I’ve seen situations where a micromanager or disengaged remote manager creates bottlenecks, but pushing back directly feels risky, and going to HR feels like a nuclear option with unclear outcomes.

So where is the actual line between a situation that can be fixed by better “managing up” (e.g., clearer communication, setting expectations) versus one that requires escalation or even disengagement? For example, if a manager’s behavior is consistently harmful but not clearly “unethical,” does relying on HR or formal structures realistically solve the issue—or does Chapter 9 underestimate how often these systems fail in practice?


More broadly, are these “special cases” really exceptions, or do they expose the limits of the entire managing-up framework when power imbalances and organizational incentives don’t support change?
